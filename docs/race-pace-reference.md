## 1. Sprint Decay Curves: 200m & 400m

### Physiology

The 400m is a **speed-endurance** event. Energy contribution breaks down roughly as:

| Phase | Distance | Primary System |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| Start–50m | 0–50m | ATP-CP (alactic) |
| 50–250m | 50–250m | Anaerobic glycolytic (lactic) |
| 250–400m | 250–400m | Aerobic (30–40% of total energy in final 150m) |

A runner reaches peak velocity around **50–60m** in a pure sprint; in the 400m, peak velocity is reached around **100–150m**, after which deceleration begins. No human can maintain maximal sprint velocity beyond approximately 50–60m before physiological fatigue sets in. The goal of speed-endurance training is to **reduce the rate of deceleration** in the final 100m — not necessarily to run the first 200m faster.

**The "Crossover" Point:** Approximately 30–35 seconds into a 400m race, the anaerobic alactic system begins significant depletion, forcing a heavy shift to reliance on fast-glycolytic pathways. This is the physiological trigger for the "final 100m breakdown." Elite athletes excel because they maintain higher stride frequencies for longer durations under high lactic acid concentrations — ground contact time increases and stride frequency drops as fatigue accumulates.

### Expected 200m–400m Differential

For a well-paced 400m the second 200m is typically **1.5–2.5 seconds slower** than the first 200m. Elite 400m runners (Hanon & Gajer, 2009) run the first 200m roughly **4–6% faster** than the second 200m.

| Goal 400m | 1st 200m | 2nd 200m | Differential |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|
| 50.0s     | 23.5–24.0s | 26.0–26.5s | ~2.5s |
| 52.0s     | 24.5–25.0s | 27.0–27.5s | ~2.5s |
| 54.0s     | 25.5–26.0s | 28.0–28.5s | ~2.5s |
| 56.0s     | 26.5–27.0s | 29.0–29.5s | ~2.5s |

### 100m Split Projections

Using elite-models from BrianMac, PMC research, and World Athletics biomechanics analyses, a well-paced 400m distributes total time across 100m segments at roughly these percentages:

| Segment | % of Total Time | 50.0s | 52.0s | 54.0s | 56.0s |
|---------|:-:|:-----:|:-----:|:-----:|:-----:|
| **0–100m**   | ~25.4% | 12.7s | 13.2s | 13.7s | 14.2s |
| **100–200m** | ~23.3% | 11.7s | 12.1s | 12.6s | 13.0s |
| **200–300m** | ~24.4% | 12.2s | 12.7s | 13.2s | 13.7s |
| **300–400m** | ~26.9% | 13.4s | 14.0s | 14.5s | 15.1s |

**Note:** The 100–200m segment is typically the fastest because the athlete has already accelerated off the blocks and is maintaining top speed. The final 100m shows the steepest deceleration — up to **13–15% slower** than the fastest segment. These are elite-model estimates; individual runners may vary based on speed vs. endurance strengths.

**The "Float":** Inexperienced 400m runners often start the first 100m at maximal (100%) intensity. Coaching literature emphasizes the backstretch "float" — a relaxed speed-maintenance phase from 100–200m where the runner preserves form without straining. This reserves capacity for the demanding final 100m, where maintaining stride mechanics under acidosis is the decisive factor.

### 200m Pacing

The 200m is a purer sprint. The second 100m is typically faster or nearly equal to the first because the first half includes the block start and acceleration phase.

| Goal 200m | 0–100m | 100–200m | Notes |
|-----------|:------:|:--------:|-------|
| 22.0s     | 11.0s  | 11.0s    | Even split possible for elite |
| 24.0s     | 11.8s  | 12.2s    | Slight positive split |
| 26.0s     | 12.6s  | 13.4s    | Larger decay |
| 28.0s     | 13.4s  | 14.6s    | Typical for developing sprinters |

For most athletes the second 100m is **0.5–1.5s slower** than the first 100m. The most common error in 200m racing is "speed tightening" — attempting to force muscle contraction through tension rather than relaxation in the final 50m, which actually impairs power output and increases deceleration.

## 2. Even vs. Negative Split Strategies

### By Distance

| Distance | Best Strategy for a Time | Championship (Tactical) | Why |
|----------|-------------------------|------------------------|-----|
| **400m** | Positive split | Positive split | Anaerobic-dominant; speed decay is physiologically unavoidable. Elite runners run first 200m ~4–6% faster (Hanon & Gajer, 2009). |
| **800m** | Slight positive split (~49/51 rule) | Positive split or sit-and-kick | First lap ~1–3s faster than second; ~90% of world records are positive-split. The race sits at the border of anaerobic and aerobic. |
| **1500m/Mile** | U-shaped (fast start/end, settle middle) | Sit-and-kick → negative split | Record attempts use even-to-U; tactical championship races often negative-split because of conservative early pacing. |
| **5K** | Even or slightly positive | Negative split (kick-driven) | Even pacing is most efficient for time trials; tactical races often slow-start, fast-finish. Elite pacing is actually "U-shaped" per Coach Saltmarsh. |

### What "Optimal" Looks Like

#### 400m — Positive Split (Inevitable)

Attempting an "even" pace in a 400m is physiologically impossible for all-out efforts. The goal is not to avoid deceleration but to **manage the rate of decay**. Performance in the final 100m is highly correlated with the athlete's Reactive Strength Index (RSI) and Rate of Force Development (RFD).

#### 800m — The 49/51 Rule

The first lap should be roughly **49% of total time**, the second lap **51%**. In practice, this means the first 400m is 1–3 seconds faster.

| Goal | 400m Split | Second 400m | Strategy |
|:----:|:----------:|:-----------:|----------|
| 1:48  | ~52.5s     | ~55.5s      | Controlled aggression; avoid sprinting first 300m |
| 2:00  | ~58s       | ~62s         | Even effort; don't get boxed in |
| 2:12  | ~64s       | ~68s         | Settle after 200m, push final 200m |

**Key insight:** For breakthrough performances, runners are often encouraged to accept the discomfort of the second lap rather than holding back in the first (Coach Jay Johnson). By running the first half aggressively, the athlete challenges their body to maintain pace under higher levels of systemic acidosis during the final 400m.

#### 1500m/Mile — U-Shaped

Fast start to establish position, settle in the middle laps, then a progressive pick-up in the final 400–600m.

| Goal (Mile) | Opening 400m | Middle 800m | Final 400m |
|:-----------:|:------------:|:-----------:|:----------:|
| 4:00        | ~57s         | ~2:02       | ~61s       |
| 4:30        | ~64s         | ~2:18       | ~68s       |
| 5:00        | ~71s         | ~2:34       | ~75s       |

The "third-lap problem" — where pace drops significantly on the penultimate lap — is the primary barrier to faster times in championship racing. Strategies like "chunking" (breaking the race into segments) help maintain intensity through this critical phase.

#### 5K — Even, With a U-Shaped Nuance

| Goal | Per-km Split | Strategy |
|:----:|:-----------:|----------|
| 15:00 | 3:00/km     | Even; first km no faster than 2:58 |
| 18:00 | 3:36/km     | Slightly conservative first km, building through the race |
| 20:00 | 4:00/km     | Negative split possible; reserve energy for final 2km |

**Note on "U-shaped" pacing:** Elite 5K pacing is not perfectly flat. The gun start provides a burst of alactic energy (ATP-CP) that should be used to establish position, followed by a "settle" into rhythm, and a progressive acceleration over the final kilometer. The middle kilometers are where most runners "back off" at the first sign of discomfort — the critical skill is maintaining intensity through this discomfort zone.

### When Are Negative Splits Optimal?

True negative splitting (running the second half faster) is rare in track events shorter than the marathon. It is typically a **consequence of conservative racing** or tactical circumstances rather than a physiological requirement for peak performance:

- **800m:** Almost never optimal for time trials. Only occurs in sit-and-kick championship races.
- **1500m/Mile:** Can work in tactical races where the early pace is slow and the race becomes a kicker's affair.
- **5K:** Viable for developing runners who lack confidence in their pace judgment; starting conservatively and building avoids the catastrophic positive split.

## 3. Common Pacing Mistakes

### 200m
| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Going all-out from the gun | Ties up in final 40m; huge deceleration | Build through the drive phase (0–30m), then accelerate through the curve |
| Improper curve lean | Braking force on the bend | Align hips to the curve; don't over-lean |
| "Speed tightening" in final 50m | Tension impairs power output, increasing deceleration | Stay relaxed; maintain arm mechanics rather than forcing stride |

### 400m
| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Blinding the first 200m at 100% effort | Massive deceleration in final 100m (the "400m wall") | First 200m at ~90–95% effort; controlled speed |
| Fighting fatigue with tension in the backstretch | Ties up completely before the final straight | "Float" the backstretch — maintain velocity without straining |
| Poor bend running | Extra distance run (wide lane) | Stay on the rail; lean into the curve |
| Neglecting the 100–200m "float" phase | Burns anaerobic reserves needed for the final 100m | Treat 100–200m as a relaxed speed-maintenance phase |

### 800m
| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Going out too fast to secure position | Lactate spike by 500m; survival-mode finish | Even or slightly positive split (e.g., 52/54 target) |
| Surging in the first 300m | Premature fatigue; "the tie-up" | Settle after 200m; wait for the final 200m to move |
| Getting boxed in on the rail | Trapped when the kick starts | Secure position within first 100–200m; avoid drifting to Lane 2 |
| Premature surging between 200–300m | Burns reserves for the critical 600–700m window | Maintain composure; trust the pace until the "critical move" window |
| Drifting wide on curves | Runs extra distance, wastes energy | Hold the rail; only pass on straights when possible |

### 1500m / Mile
| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Settling into a slow, comfortable pace | Too far back to close when leaders kick | Take controlled risks early; don't over-conserve |
| Uneven splits from reacting to every move | Higher energy cost than even pacing | Run your own race plan; let others surge and resettle |
| Forgetting the bell lap | Left with too much ground to close | Know where you need to be at the bell; execute that position |
| Being too far back at the bell | Tactical cost to accelerate past the field is prohibitive | Stay within top 3–4 positions entering the final lap |

### 5K
| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Going out too fast from adrenaline | Drastic slowdown in km 3–4 | First km should feel almost too easy |
| Obsessing over even splits | Often leads to a positive split (compromises final km) | Accept a slight positive; finish hard |
| Starting the kick too late | Leaves time on the table | Start the move at 1.2–1.5km to go, not 400m |
| Mismanaging terrain (hills) | Premature localized muscle fatigue | Pace by **effort**, not by watch — don't fight to hold even pace on inclines; don't brake on descents |
| Backing off at first discomfort (the "crucible") | Never learns to race through the middle kilometers | Maintain intensity through km 3; the kick is the byproduct of arriving at 4.6km with reserves |

**Sources:**
- [High School Race Strategy](https://coachsaltmarsh.com/high-school-race-strategy/) — Coach Saltmarsh
- [The Speed Project: How To Run The 200m](https://www.thespeedproject.com/how-to-run-200m/)

## 4. Pace Conversion Formulas

### Key Constants
- **1 mile = 1.609344 km**
- **1 km = 1,000 m**
- Track lane 1 (standard): 400m per lap

### Formulas

#### min/mile ↔ min/km

```
min/km = min/mile ÷ 1.609344
min/mile = min/km × 1.609344
```

**Worked example** — 8:00 min/mile:
```
1. 8:00 = 480 seconds
2. 480 ÷ 1.609344 = 298.3 seconds/km
3. 298.3 ÷ 60 = 4.97 minutes → 4:58 min/km
```

**Worked example** — 5:00 min/km:
```
1. 5:00 = 300 seconds
2. 300 × 1.609344 = 482.8 seconds/mile
3. 482.8 ÷ 60 = 8.046 minutes → 8:03 min/mile
```

#### min/km ↔ seconds per 100m (track pace)

```
sec/100m = (min/km in seconds) ÷ 10
min/km = (sec/100m × 10) seconds → convert to min:sec
```

**Worked example** — 4:00 min/km:
```
1. 4:00 = 240 seconds/km
2. 240 ÷ 10 = 24 seconds per 100m
```

**Worked example** — 30 seconds per 100m:
```
1. 30 × 10 = 300 seconds/km
2. 300 ÷ 60 = 5:00 min/km
```

#### Lap time for a given pace

```
lap_seconds = sec_per_100m × 4
```

**Examples:**
- 24s/100m → 96s per lap → 1:36 per 400m lap
- 30s/100m → 120s per lap → 2:00 per 400m lap
- 20s/100m → 80s per lap → 1:20 per 400m lap

### Track-to-Road Adjustments

There is no single universal formula to convert track times to road times because road courses vary in elevation, turns, and surface. However, the following methods are widely used:

#### World Athletics Scoring Tables
The gold standard for comparing performances across events. These are complex equations that assign "points" to any performance. By calculating the points for a track time, you can find the equivalent road time that equates to the same point value. Available at: [World Athletics Scoring Tables](https://worldathletics.org/records/certified-roadevents)

#### Riegel's Formula (Performance Prediction)
Used to predict equivalent performances across distances:
```
t₂ = t₁ × (d₂ / d₁)^1.06
```
Where t = time and d = distance. While primarily used for predicting race times at different distances, this serves as a baseline for road-to-track comparisons.

#### Surface/Environment Factors
- **5K Road vs. Track:** Analysis suggests road races are roughly **1.0–1.6% slower** than track equivalents for men; women may see smaller or negligible differences due to pacing dynamics (e.g., pacers in road races).
- **Course Certification:** World Athletics only recognizes times for records if the road course is officially certified. Elevation drop, net downhill, and point-to-point courses all affect record eligibility.

### Hand-Timing Adjustment (Track Standards)

When comparing hand-timed results to Fully Automatic Timing (FAT) results:

| Race Distance | Adjustment Added to Hand Time |
|:---|:---|
| **Under 300m** | +0.24 seconds |
| **300m to <800m** | +0.14 seconds |
| **800m and above** | 0.00 seconds (rounding only) |

**Important:** Before adding these factors, always **round the hand time up** to the next tenth of a second first (e.g., 10.83 → 10.9, then add 0.24 → 11.14).
