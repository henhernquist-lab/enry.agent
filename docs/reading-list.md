# Reading List

> Curated for a 9th-grade student-athlete who builds AI apps,
> runs track, lifts, reads seriously, and wants to think well.
>
> 15 essays. No listicles. No hype. Only things worth rereading.

---

## AI / Tech (4)

### Software 2.0
**Andrej Karpathy** · [karpathy.medium.com](https://karpathy.medium.com/software-2-0-a64152b37c35)

The essay that defines the paradigm shift: neural networks aren't just a new tool, they're a new way to write software — by curating data instead of writing logic. Reading this first makes everything else about AI make more sense.

### Stuff we figured out about AI in 2023
**Simon Willison** · [simonwillison.net](https://simonwillison.net/2023/Dec/31/ai-in-2023/)

The clearest, most honest annual review of what LLMs can and can't do, written by someone who actually builds with them. Covers prompt injection, running models locally, and why "vibes-based development" is the current state of the art.

### Tracing the thoughts of a large language model
**Anthropic** · [anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/research/tracing-thoughts-language-model)

A rare look inside the black box — Anthropic's interpretability team shows how neural circuits actually handle translation, rhyming, and math. Concrete evidence that model behavior is rooted in real (if complex) mathematical pathways, not magic.

### Building effective agents
**Anthropic** · [anthropic.com](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/building-effective-agents)

A grounding manual for anyone building AI-powered tools. Distinguishes between workflows (predefined code paths) and agents (models using tools dynamically), with practical patterns for routing, parallelization, and evaluator loops. The section on treating tool definitions as "agent-computer interfaces" is worth the read alone.

## Training / Athletics (3)

### What speed training really means
**Juggernaut Training Systems** · [jtsstrength.com](https://www.jtsstrength.com/what-speed-training-really-means/)

The single most important principle for a young track athlete: to get faster, you must sprint at 95%+ of max velocity. Running while tired teaches your body to move slowly. Explains why quality and rest matter more than volume.

### Periodization: what the data say
**Stronger by Science** · [strongerbyscience.com](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/periodization-data/)

Evidence-based breakdown of how to structure training over time — cycling between volume, intensity, and recovery phases. Essential for anyone writing their own training plan or trying to understand why their coach programs what they do.

### Recovery techniques for athletes
**Gatorade Sports Science Institute** · [gssiweb.org](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-120-recovery-techniques-for-athletes)

Sorts the recovery science from the bro-science. Bottom line: sleep and nutrition are the only non-negotiable recovery tools. Ice baths, compression, and active recovery are helpful but secondary. Adolescent athletes need 8–10 hours — and most don't get it.

## Thinking / Writing (3)

### How to think for yourself
**Paul Graham** · [paulgraham.com](https://www.paulgraham.com/think.html)

Graham argues that independent-mindedness isn't just intelligence — it's a specific fastidiousness about truth, combined with the willingness to ignore what everyone else thinks. The kind of essay that changes how you read everything else.

### Book review: The Scout Mindset
**Scott Alexander (Astral Codex Ten)** · [astralcodexten.com](https://www.astralcodexten.com/p/book-review-the-scout-mindset)

A review that stands on its own as an essay about motivated reasoning — the difference between wanting to be right (soldier mindset) and wanting to see reality clearly (scout mindset). Essential for anyone who wants to make good decisions instead of comfortable ones.

### Why and how to write things on the Internet
**Ben Kuhn** · [benkuhn.net](https://www.benkuhn.net/writing/)

Practical advice on using writing as a thinking tool. Kuhn makes the case that lowering the bar for publishing is the fastest way to develop clarity, and gives concrete guidance on how to start. If you're building in public or just want to think better, start here.

## Building / Product (3)

### Do things that don't scale
**Paul Graham** · [paulgraham.com](https://www.paulgraham.com/ds.html)

The canonical essay for anyone building something from scratch. Graham argues that manual, unscalable effort (recruiting users one-by-one, providing white-glove service) is the fastest path to building something people actually want. The Stripe story — the "Collison installation" — is worth the read alone.

### Frighteningly ambitious startup ideas
**Paul Graham** · [paulgraham.com](https://www.paulgraham.com/ambitious.html)

The best ideas often seem terrifying. Graham's framework: live in the future, notice what's missing, and build the thing you wish existed. Don't worry about the size of the market — worry about whether you can solve a real problem for a specific group of people.

### Patrick Collison — essays & writing
**Patrick Collison (Stripe co-founder)** · [patrickcollison.com](https://patrickcollison.com/)

Not a single essay but a collection of writing and interviews about craftsmanship, pace, and building things that last. Collison's emphasis on the quality of interfaces and the beauty of well-maintained code is a masterclass for anyone who wants their work to communicate that they care.

## General (2)

### On keeping a notebook
**Joan Didion** · [PDF](https://cdn.thewirecutter.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/Joan-Didion-On-Keeping-a-Notebook.pdf)

The best essay ever written about why we write things down. Didion argues that the point of a notebook isn't to record facts — it's to record how things felt and why they mattered. Teaches you to be more observant and to trust that your own experience is worth examining.

### Once more to the lake
**E. B. White** · [PDF](http://www.davidglensmith.com/wcjc/1301/PDFs/white-demo.pdf)

A man returns to the lake he visited as a boy, now with his own son. A quiet, devastating essay about time, memory, and the shock of realizing you've become the adult. Reads like a short story. Best read in one sitting, slowly.
